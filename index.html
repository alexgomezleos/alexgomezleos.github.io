I enjoy studying complexity bounds for algorithmic problems.

Lately, I've been interested in lower bounds for low memory / communication models. I think its fascinating when a problem transitions from "efficiently solvable" to "completely intractable" upon relatively modest resource constraints. A great example is this. I feel these types of results give valuable insight to the following meta-question: A common research thrust is to adapt known algorithms to increasingly stringent requirements, such as privacy or the ability to parallelize. Can we really tweak the "textbook algorithm" to fit all our needs, or is it already pareto-optimal for basic resources?

I'm also interested in sublinear algorithms, mechanism design, and fair division. Topics I've spent more time thinking about includes bandit algorithms, MDPs, distribution testing, and low-rank tensor estimation.

Publications

  • Simple and Nearly-Optimal Sampling for Rank-1 Tensor Completion via Gauss-Jordan
    with Oscar Lopez
    In review.
    Summary: We describe a simple two-way reduction of rank-1 tensor completion to solving a pair of random linear systems. This latter problem lets us bound the number of observations required to solve the original problem.
  • Single-Server Private Information Retrieval With Side Information Under Arbitrary Popularity Profiles
    with Anoosheh Heidarzadeh
    Presented at Information Theory Workshop (ITW), 2022. (slides)
    Summary: Suppose you want to obtain some item from a database without revealing any information about the identity of the item. Before this work, there were protocols that use ideas from linear coding. However, they relied on the assumption that each item is equally likely to be queried. By randomizing previously known protocols, we relaxed this latter assumption. The key technical ingredient was proving an extremal property holding for sufficiently large databases.
